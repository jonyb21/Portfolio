# Hinged D-Lock Design

## Outcome

Replace the folding lock with a compact, realistic D-lock that looks considered, reads clearly in use, and mounts neatly to a bicycle frame.

## Selected architecture

Use a rigid hardened-steel D-shaped shackle with one leg permanently captured by a concealed pivot inside a compact keyed crossbar. The opposite leg swings into a recessed receiver and is secured by an internal deadbolt. Unlocking releases only the receiver end, so the product remains one assembly with no loose shackle or crossbar.

The pivot and receiver are deliberately different: the pivot end has a circular knuckle integrated into the body, while the locking end has a narrow rectangular entry slot and a visible stainless-steel collar. This makes the operating sequence understandable from a single photograph.

## Form and materials

- Slim rounded-rectangle crossbar in bead-blasted dark aluminium.
- Desaturated blue moulded elastomer sleeve over the hardened-steel shackle.
- Small stainless-steel receiver collar and concealed pivot pin.
- Recessed key cylinder on the body end with a thin cool-white LED ring.
- No exposed screws, decorative fasteners, fake seams, removable caps, or unexplained controls.

## Carry system

A low-profile black frame mount clamps to the bicycle seat tube. The closed D-lock slides vertically into two rubber-lined guides and clicks into a spring latch on the crossbar. A single thumb tab releases it. The mount is separate from the security mechanism and never appears to lock the bicycle.

## Alternatives considered

- Conventional removable-shackle U-lock: mechanically proven, but creates two loose pieces and repeats a generic market form.
- Retractable cable lock: compact, but visually communicates lower security and gives less opportunity for a precise industrial design statement.
- Hinged D-lock: selected because it is secure-looking, mechanically legible, portable, and remains one complete object.
